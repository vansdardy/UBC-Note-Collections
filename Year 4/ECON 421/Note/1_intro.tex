\section{Introduction}
Before we introduce what game theory is about, we first clarify what game theory is not about. Game theory is NOT about competitive markets. \\
Game theory evaluates strategic situations in which we analyze players' best courses of actions depending on opponents which lead to a Nash equilibrium or another form of equilibrium. Some classic examples are \impt{prisoner's dilemma} and the \impt{penalty kick}. \\
There are many forms of a game. This include \impt{dynamic games} where players move at different times, \impt{games of perfect information} which leads to \impt{Zermelo's theorem}, \impt{bargaining}, \impt{signalling}, \impt{reputation games}, and \impt{auctions}. \\
In the following note, we go through them case by case where we give relevant definitions, analyses, and explanations.
